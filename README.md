## 3. Compare and contrast Agile and Waterfall methodologies. What are the main advantages and disadvantages of each?
Comparison of Agile and Waterfall Methodologies
Agile has iterative and incremental approach while waterfall has sequential and linear approach
Agile has high flexibility where changes are easily incorporated while waterfall has low flexibility where changes are costly and difficult
Agile folows Cycles Project Phases through iterations (sprints)	approach while waterfall follows a strict phase-by-phase approach
Agile testing occurs throughout development while waterfall	testing occurs only after the development phase is completed
Agile is best suited for complex, evolving projects while waterfall is best suited well-defined, structured projects
Advantages and Disadvantages of Agile vs. Waterfall
Agile Methodology
Advantages:
Faster Delivery â€“ Frequent releases ensure continuous product improvement (Schwaber & Sutherland, 2020).
Customer Satisfaction â€“ Regular feedback ensures that the final product aligns with user expectations (Larman, 2019).

Disadvantages:

Difficult to Predict Costs and Time â€“ Since requirements evolve, estimating final costs and timelines is challenging (Gido & Clements, 2021).
Not Ideal for All Projects â€“ Works best for projects with uncertain requirements, but may not be suitable for fixed-budget projects (Somerville, 2020).
Waterfall Methodology
Advantages:
Clear Structure â€“ Well-defined phases make planning and execution straightforward (PMBOK Guide, 2021).
Easy to Manage â€“ Progress tracking is simpler as each phase is completed before moving to the next (Kerzner, 2022).
Good for Fixed Requirements â€“ Best suited for projects with well-defined scope and minimal changes (Satzinger, Jackson, & Burd, 2019).
Predictable Budget and Timeline â€“ Costs and schedules are set in advance, making financial planning easier (Somerville, 2020).
Disadvantages:
Inflexibility â€“ Changes are difficult to implement once a phase is completed (Pressman & Maxim, 2020).
Late Testing â€“ Errors may only be detected at the end of the project, leading to costly rework (PMI, 2021).
Limited Client Interaction â€“ Lack of continuous feedback increases the risk of delivering a product that does not meet user needs (Schwaber & Sutherland, 2020).

Project scope defines the boundaries, deliverables, objectives, and constraints of a project. Properly defining the scope is crucial for:

What is a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)?
A Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) is a hierarchical decomposition of a project into smaller, manageable tasks or deliverables (PMI, 2021).
Key Characteristics of a WBS:
Breaks the project into phases, deliverables, and tasks
Uses a tree structure to show task relationships
Each level represents smaller and more specific components
Helps in assigning responsibilities and tracking progress
Example of a WBS for a Website Development Project
Project Initiation
Define project scope
Gather requirements
Identify stakeholders
Design Phase
Create wireframes and UI/UX prototypes
Develop design specifications
Development Phase
Frontend development
Backend development
Database setup
Testing Phase
Unit testing
Integration testing
User acceptance testing
Deployment & Maintenance
Launch website
Provide post-launch support
Why is a WBS Useful?
1. Improves Project Organization â€“ Breaks complex projects into smaller, manageable tasks (Kerzner, 2022).
2. Enhances Time & Cost Estimation â€“ Helps in better scheduling and budgeting by identifying all tasks upfront (Gido & Clements, 2021).
3. Assigns Clear Responsibilities â€“ Ensures accountability by assigning tasks to specific teams or individuals (Pressman & Maxim, 2020).
4. Reduces Risks â€“ Identifies potential bottlenecks early and allows for proactive risk management (PMI, 2021).
5. Facilitates Progress Tracking â€“ Makes it easier to monitor project milestones and deliverables (Somerville, 2020).
